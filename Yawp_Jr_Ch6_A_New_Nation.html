Chapter Overview

After the American Revolution, the new United States faced a huge question: How do you actually run a country? The Articles of Confederation‚Äîthe nation's first set of rules for government‚Äîwere too weak to hold the states together. Farmers rebelled, debts piled up, and leaders argued about what kind of government the country should have.

This chapter tells the story of how Americans created the Constitution, formed the first political parties, and tested the limits of their new democracy. It was a messy, controversial, and sometimes violent process‚Äîbut it built the foundation for the government we still have today.

Big Questions for This Chapter

  1. Why did the Articles of Confederation fail, and what replaced them?
  2. What compromises shaped the Constitution‚Äîand who was left out?
  3. Why did Americans disagree so strongly about how the new government should work?
  4. How did early conflicts over power, money, and liberty shape the country we live in today?

I. Introduction

On July 4, 1788, the people of Philadelphia held a "grand federal procession" to celebrate the new Constitution. Blacksmiths carried a working forge and hammered swords into farm tools. Potters held a sign connecting a craftsman's skill to a citizen's power. Christian ministers marched arm-in-arm with Jewish leaders. It was a powerful image of what Americans hoped their country would become: diverse, united, and free.

But the reality was messier than the parade. The new nation was deeply divided. The Constitution itself had been controversial. Less than two years before those celebrations, the country had nearly fallen apart. This chapter is the story of those struggles‚Äîand how Americans fought, debated, and compromised their way into becoming a nation.

II. Shays's Rebellion

üìñ Vocabulary: Shays's Rebellion
Articles of Confederation
‚Äî The first set of rules for the U.S. government (1781‚Äì1789), which gave very little power to the national government.
Foreclose
‚Äî When a lender takes someone's property because they can't pay their debts.
Rebellion
‚Äî An organized fight against the people in power.
Treason
‚Äî The crime of betraying your country.
Nationalist
‚Äî Someone who wanted a stronger central (national) government.

In 1786 and 1787, thousands of farmers in western Massachusetts were drowning in debt. The economy was weak, and the national government under the Articles of Confederation could do almost nothing to help. The Articles gave Congress no power to collect taxes, and the states were left to deal with war debts on their own.

When Massachusetts sided with the wealthy lenders instead of the struggling farmers, many of those farmers‚Äîincluding veterans of the Revolutionary War‚Äîdecided to fight back. Led by a veteran named Daniel Shays, they blocked courthouses so judges couldn't order their farms to be taken away. They saw themselves as carrying on the spirit of the Revolution: standing up for their rights.

But the governor of Massachusetts saw them as dangerous rebels. He sent thousands of militia troops to crush the uprising. Over a thousand people were arrested, and Shays himself was charged with treason‚Äîthough he was eventually pardoned.

üîç MULTIPLE PERSPECTIVES: Was Shays's Rebellion justified?
The Shaysites (farmers): They were protecting their rights, just like the patriots had done during the Revolution. The government was failing them.
Thomas Jefferson: "A little rebellion now and then" is healthy for democracy. People should be able to push back when government is not working.
James Madison: This proved the country needed a much stronger national government. Without one, liberty itself was in danger.
Governor Bowdoin: These were violent mobs threatening law and order. The government must be able to keep the peace.
Key Idea
Shays's Rebellion showed that the Articles of Confederation were too weak to handle the young nation's problems. It became a major reason why leaders pushed for a new, stronger Constitution.

  1. Were the Shaysites more like the patriots of 1776 or dangerous rebels? What evidence supports your answer?
  2. Why might veterans of the Revolution feel especially frustrated by the government's response?

III. The Constitutional Convention

üìñ Vocabulary: The Constitutional Convention
Convention
‚Äî A formal meeting where people come together to discuss and make decisions.
Virginia Plan
‚Äî James Madison's proposal for a strong national government with representation based on population.
New Jersey Plan
‚Äî William Paterson's proposal that kept equal representation for each state, like under the Articles.
Great Compromise
‚Äî The agreement that created two houses of Congress‚Äîone based on population (House), one with equal representation (Senate).
Electoral College
‚Äî A special group of electors chosen to pick the president, rather than having the people vote directly.
Three-Fifths Compromise
‚Äî The agreement to count each enslaved person as three-fifths of a person for purposes of representation and taxes.

Shays's Rebellion convinced leaders across the country that something had to change. In the summer of 1787, delegates from twelve of the thirteen states gathered in Philadelphia to fix the Articles of Confederation. (Rhode Island refused to participate.) But James Madison of Virginia had bigger plans‚Äîhe wanted to replace the Articles entirely.

Madison brought a bold proposal called the Virginia Plan. It called for a strong national government with three branches: a legislature (Congress) to make laws, an executive (president) to enforce them, and a judiciary (courts) to interpret them. Representation in Congress would be based on each state's population, which meant bigger states would have more power.

Smaller states pushed back with the New Jersey Plan, which would keep equal votes for every state‚Äîjust like under the Articles. The debate was fierce. Finally, Roger Sherman of Connecticut proposed a compromise: Congress would have two chambers. The House of Representatives would be based on population, while the Senate would give every state two votes. This became known as the Great Compromise.

But there was a darker side to these negotiations. Southern states wanted their enslaved populations to count toward their representation in Congress, even though enslaved people couldn't vote. The result was the Three-Fifths Compromise: each enslaved person would count as three-fifths of a person for representation and taxes. This gave slaveholding states extra political power.

The delegates also struggled over how to choose a president. Some feared putting too much power in one person's hands‚Äîafter all, they had just fought a revolution against a king. After months of debate, they settled on the Electoral College system.

Key Idea
The Constitution was built through compromise. Every major decision‚Äîhow Congress would work, how the president would be chosen, how enslaved people would be counted‚Äîinvolved heated debate and trade-offs. Not everyone benefited equally from these compromises.
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. Why was it so hard for large states and small states to agree on how Congress should work?
  2. The Three-Fifths Compromise gave slaveholding states more power in Congress. Who benefited from this, and who was harmed?

IV. Ratifying the Constitution

üìñ Vocabulary: Ratification
Ratify
‚Äî To officially approve something, like a law or constitution.
Federalists
‚Äî People who supported the new Constitution and a strong national government.
Anti-Federalists
‚Äî People who opposed the Constitution because they feared it gave too much power to the national government.
Bill of Rights
‚Äî The first ten amendments to the Constitution, which protect individual freedoms like speech, religion, and fair trials.
The Federalist Papers
‚Äî A series of essays by Hamilton, Madison, and Jay arguing in favor of the Constitution.

Writing the Constitution was only half the battle. Now it had to be approved by the states. This kicked off one of the biggest public debates in American history.

Those who supported the Constitution called themselves Federalists. They argued the country needed a strong national government to survive. Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay wrote a series of persuasive essays‚ÄîThe Federalist Papers‚Äîto convince people to support ratification.

Opponents, called Anti-Federalists, feared the Constitution gave the federal government too much power. Their biggest complaint? The Constitution had no Bill of Rights‚Äîno list of guaranteed freedoms to protect ordinary citizens from government overreach.

The debate played out in newspapers, churches, and coffeehouses across the country. State by state, conventions voted on whether to approve. Many states ratified only after being promised a Bill of Rights would be added. By July 1788, enough states had voted yes. The Bill of Rights‚Äîten amendments protecting freedoms like speech, religion, and fair trials‚Äîwas added in 1791.

Key Idea
The Constitution was controversial from the start. The Bill of Rights, which many Americans now consider essential, only exists because Anti-Federalists demanded it as the price of their support.
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. The Anti-Federalists lost the ratification debate, but they got the Bill of Rights added. Does that make them winners or losers? Explain.
  2. Why might ordinary citizens have been more worried about the Constitution than political leaders were?

V. Slavery and the New Nation

üìñ Vocabulary: Slavery and Compromise
Transatlantic slave trade
‚Äî The forced transportation of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas.
Surplus
‚Äî More of something than is needed.
Haitian Revolution
‚Äî A successful revolt by enslaved people in the French colony of Haiti (1791‚Äì1804), creating the first Black republic.
Louisiana Purchase
‚Äî The 1803 deal in which the U.S. bought a huge territory from France, doubling the country's size.

There was much the Constitution did not fix. Women had no guaranteed voice in government. Many states only let wealthy property owners vote. And slavery was not only allowed‚Äîit was protected.

One of the most important compromises involved the Atlantic slave trade. Many northerners opposed it on moral grounds, but also because importing more enslaved Africans would increase the South's political power through the Three-Fifths Compromise. Meanwhile, Upper South states like Virginia actually wanted to ban imports too‚Äîbecause they already had more enslaved people than they needed and could sell them at higher prices if imports stopped.

The result was a deal: Congress would allow the slave trade to continue for twenty more years, until 1808. When the ban finally took effect, it was poorly enforced. The new federal government protected the institution of slavery even as it expanded rights for white men.

üîç WHOSE VOICES WERE LEFT OUT?
Enslaved people: Had no say in the Constitution, yet the document counted them as three-fifths of a person and protected the slave trade for twenty more years.
Women: Received no specific rights or protections. Many states barred them from voting entirely.
Poor white men: Many states required property ownership to vote, shutting out large numbers of citizens.
Native Americans: Were not considered citizens and had no representation in the new government that would increasingly take their land.
Key Idea
The Constitution expanded democracy for some Americans while deliberately excluding others. Understanding who was left out‚Äîand why‚Äîis essential to understanding the full story of the new nation.
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. Why did so many different groups‚Äîboth for and against slavery‚Äîagree to the slave trade compromise?
  2. When we say the Constitution was built on compromise, who usually had to compromise the most?

VI. Hamilton's Financial Plan

üìñ Vocabulary: Hamilton's Plan
Secretary of the Treasury
‚Äî The government official in charge of the country's money and finances.
Assumption (of debt)
‚Äî When the federal government agreed to take on (pay back) the debts that states owed from the Revolutionary War.
Bank of the United States
‚Äî A national bank created to manage the country's money, make loans, and print paper currency.
Excise tax
‚Äî A tax on the production or sale of a specific good, like whiskey.
Unconstitutional
‚Äî Not allowed by the Constitution.

President George Washington's first cabinet immediately revealed the country's divisions. Alexander Hamilton, the Secretary of the Treasury, wanted an active government that supported business and industry. Thomas Jefferson, the Secretary of State, wanted a smaller government and an economy based on farming. Their disagreement would define American politics for years to come.

Hamilton proposed an ambitious plan. First, the federal government would take on all the states' unpaid war debts. This would tie the states‚Äîand especially their wealthy creditors‚Äîto the success of the national government. Second, he wanted to create a Bank of the United States to manage the country's money and loans.

Jefferson argued the bank was unconstitutional‚Äîthe Constitution didn't specifically say Congress could create one. Hamilton argued back that the Constitution gave Congress the power to do what was "necessary and proper" to run the government, and a bank was necessary. Washington sided with Hamilton.

To pay for all of this, Hamilton also proposed new taxes‚Äîincluding a tax on whiskey. For farmers on the western frontier who turned their grain into whiskey to sell, it felt like the government was picking their pockets.

Key Idea
Hamilton and Jefferson had fundamentally different visions for America. Hamilton wanted a country built on business, banks, and industry. Jefferson wanted a nation of independent farmers. This disagreement became the basis for America's first political parties.
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. Hamilton believed tying wealthy people's interests to the government would help everyone. Do you agree with that logic? Why or why not?
  2. The debate over the Bank of the United States was really about how to read the Constitution. Should the government only do what the Constitution specifically says, or can it do anything the Constitution doesn't specifically forbid?

VII. The Whiskey Rebellion and Jay's Treaty

üìñ Vocabulary: Rebellion and Foreign Relations
Impressment
‚Äî The practice of forcing sailors into military service, especially by the British navy kidnapping American sailors.
Neutrality
‚Äî Refusing to take sides in a conflict between other countries.
Jay's Treaty
‚Äî A 1794 agreement with Britain that avoided war but angered many Americans.

Hamilton's whiskey tax hit western farmers hard. For many, turning grain into whiskey was the most profitable way to sell their crops. The tax felt designed to help eastern merchants at the expense of frontier families.

In 1791, protesters in western Pennsylvania tarred and feathered a tax collector. By 1794, an armed force of about seven thousand farmers attacked federal officials and burned tax collectors' homes. President Washington responded by leading thirteen thousand militia troops westward‚Äîthe only time a sitting president has personally led troops in the field. The rebels scattered.

The Whiskey Rebellion proved the federal government could enforce its laws‚Äîsomething impossible under the Articles. But it also showed that many Americans already saw the new government as their enemy rather than their protector.

Meanwhile, Britain and France were at war, and both interfered with American trade. British ships seized American sailors (impressment) and forced them into the British navy. Jay's Treaty, signed in 1794, got Britain to leave its western forts and pay for some shipping losses‚Äîbut it failed to stop impressment and committed the U.S. to treating Britain as its top trade partner. Republicans saw this as a betrayal, and the debate helped solidify America's first two political parties.

Key Idea
Both the Whiskey Rebellion and Jay's Treaty revealed how divided Americans were‚Äîbetween East and West, rich and poor, and those who favored Britain versus those who favored France.
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. Compare the government's response to Shays's Rebellion with its response to the Whiskey Rebellion. What had changed?
  2. Why would a trade treaty with Britain become one of the most divisive issues in early American politics?

VIII. The French Revolution and the Limits of Liberty

üìñ Vocabulary: Revolution Abroad, Crisis at Home
French Revolution
‚Äî The overthrow of the French king beginning in 1789, which inspired both hope and fear in America.
Reign of Terror
‚Äî A period during the French Revolution when thousands were executed by the revolutionary government.
Alien Act
‚Äî A 1798 law allowing the government to deport immigrants considered dangerous.
Sedition Act
‚Äî A 1798 law making it illegal to criticize the president or government.
XYZ Affair
‚Äî A scandal in which French officials demanded bribes from American diplomats.
Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions
‚Äî Documents arguing that states could declare federal laws unconstitutional.

When the French Revolution began in 1789, most Americans cheered. Liberty was spreading! Towns held parades on July 14th, and people wore French-inspired clothing.

But as the revolution turned violent‚Äîwith a Reign of Terror that killed thousands, including the king and queen‚Äîmany Americans grew alarmed. Those who feared the chaos became Federalists and leaned toward Britain. Those who still supported the revolution became Republicans and leaned toward France.

Tensions with France escalated when French officials demanded bribes before negotiating with American diplomats (the XYZ Affair). In this atmosphere of fear, Congress passed two controversial laws in 1798. The Alien Act let the government deport immigrants seen as threats. The Sedition Act made it a crime to criticize the president or government‚Äîand Federalists used it to arrest Republican newspaper editors.

In response, Thomas Jefferson and James Madison secretly wrote the Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions, arguing that states could declare federal laws unconstitutional. This idea‚Äîstates' rights‚Äîwould echo through American history for decades, including in the lead-up to the Civil War.

Key Idea
The Alien and Sedition Acts raised a question Americans still debate today: In times of crisis, how much freedom should the government be able to take away in the name of security?
ü§î STOP AND THINK
  1. Should the government be allowed to punish people for criticizing it during wartime? What are the dangers of saying yes‚Äîand of saying no?
  2. Why might the same Americans who fought a revolution for liberty also support laws that restricted free speech?

IX. Religious Freedom

üìñ Vocabulary: Church and State
Established church
‚Äî An official state religion supported by tax money.
Disestablishment
‚Äî The gradual process of separating church and state‚Äîending government support for official religions.
First Amendment
‚Äî The part of the Bill of Rights that protects freedom of religion, speech, press, assembly, and petition.

In 1776, every state either had an official church supported by taxes, or required its leaders to follow a certain religion. Over the next several decades, that changed. Thomas Jefferson and James Madison argued that government support for religion was a form of oppression. Jefferson's Statute for Religious Freedom in Virginia became a model for the First Amendment, which guaranteed that Congress would not establish an official national religion.

However, the First Amendment only applied to the federal government‚Äînot the states. Many states continued to support official churches well into the 1800s. Americans continued to argue about what "separation of church and state" really meant in practice.

Key Idea
Religious freedom in America didn't happen all at once. It was a gradual process, and debates about the relationship between religion and government continue today.

X. The Election of 1800

üìñ Vocabulary: The Peaceful Transfer of Power
Judicial review
‚Äî The power of the Supreme Court to decide whether a law is constitutional.
Marbury v. Madison
‚Äî The 1803 Supreme Court case that established the principle of judicial review.
Precedent
‚Äî An action or decision that serves as an example for future situations.

By 1800, President John Adams was deeply unpopular. The Alien and Sedition Acts had backfired, and his own party leader, Alexander Hamilton, offered little support. The election of 1800 was one of the nastiest in American history‚Äîboth sides predicted disaster.

The election came down to a tie between two Republicans: Thomas Jefferson and Aaron Burr. Under the original rules, the House of Representatives had to break the tie. It took thirty-six rounds of voting before Jefferson won. (The Twelfth Amendment was later added to prevent this.)

Despite the bitterness, something remarkable happened: power transferred peacefully from one political party to another for the first time. Adams left, Jefferson took over, and no one took up arms. Jefferson called it a "bloodless revolution."

This era also produced a landmark Supreme Court case. In Marbury v. Madison (1803), Chief Justice John Marshall established judicial review‚Äîthe idea that the Supreme Court can strike down laws it considers unconstitutional. This power remains central to American government today.

Key Idea
The peaceful transfer of power in 1800 set one of the most important precedents in American democracy. The idea that the losing side accepts the result and steps aside‚Äîwithout violence‚Äîis something Americans established right at the beginning.

  2. Why is the peaceful transfer of power considered one of the most important traditions in American democracy?

XI. Wrapping Up: A Nation Built on Debate

The first years of the United States were anything but smooth. Americans fought over how strong the government should be, who should have power, how to handle money and debt, how to deal with foreign countries, and what rights the people should have. At every step, compromise was necessary‚Äîbut those compromises often came at a cost, especially for enslaved people, women, Native Americans, and the poor.

And yet, through all of this conflict, the country survived. The Constitution created a government flexible enough to adapt. The Bill of Rights protected individual freedoms. The peaceful transfer of power in 1800 proved that democracy could work without descending into chaos.

The debates of this era‚Äîabout federal power vs. states' rights, about who gets included in "We the People," about how to balance liberty and order‚Äîare not just historical. They are still the debates we have today.
